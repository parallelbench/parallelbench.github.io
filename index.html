ParallelBench

Understanding the Trade-offs of Parallel Decoding in Diffusion LLMs

Diffusion LLMs (dLLMs) promise massive speedups via parallel decoding, but their conditional-independence assumption breaks when tokens must coordinate. ParallelBench is the first benchmark designed explicitly for this failure mode. It pairs tractable theory with realistic tasks that humans and Autoregressive (AR) LLMs solve easily—but where dLLMs collapse as parallelism grows—making the speed-quality trade-off measurable and comparable across models and decoding strategies.

Core Contributions

Why ParallelBench?

Parallel decoding is fast, but not free. When outputs require token-to-token coordination, factorized predictions ignore dependencies, resulting in a drop in quality. Existing math/coding benchmarks rarely expose this. ParallelBench closes the gap with information-theoretic analysis and tasks explicitly designed to stress dependency handling.

Left: Parallel decoding mixes individually likely tokens into invalid pairs when dependencies are ignored. Middle: Closed-form analyses on list operations quantify accuracy decay as parallelism increases. Right: A realistic benchmark exposes speed–quality trade-offs across dLLMs and decoding strategies.

Information-Theoretic Analysis

Proves that parallel decoding has fundamental error bounds when tokens depend on each other, showing even perfect models struggle as we increase parallelism on tasks requiring strong token coordination.

Quantitative Case Studies

Analytically tractable synthetic list operations (Copy, Replace, Shuffle) with closed-form accuracy formulas demonstrate fundamental limitations: specific tasks show inevitable quality degradation under parallel decoding.

Realistic Benchmark Tasks

Seventeen tasks across Waiting Line, Text Writing, and Puzzles—all trivial for humans and AR LLMs—reveal severe quality degradation in dLLMs under parallel decoding in real-world scenarios.

What's Included

Benchmark Overview

We introduce ParallelBench, a comprehensive benchmark consisting of 17 realistic tasks across 3 categories, designed to systematically evaluate the speed-quality trade-offs of dLLMs.

Waiting Line

Waiting Line is a set of list operations (e.g., shuffle, replace) manipulating realistic customer service scenarios. The number of customers n serves as a controlled variable for adjusting difficulty.

Text Writing

Text Writing consists of five text generation tasks: summarization, paraphrasing, words-to-sentence generation. We introduce a Grammar Score metric that imposes stronger token-level dependencies.

View Examples
5 tasks

Puzzles

Puzzles consists of two classic puzzle tasks: Sudoku, which has a unique solution, and Latin Square, which has multiple valid solutions. This fundamental difference in solution space highlights the distinct failure modes of dLLMs.

Performance Results

Evaluation Results on ParallelBench

This section presents evaluation results of state-of-the-art dLLMs (LLaDA-1.5 and Dream 7B) on the Waiting Line benchmark, analyzing the speed-quality trade-offs in parallel decoding. For the full experimental setup and detailed results, please refer to our paper.

💡 Key Insight

Both LLaDA-1.5 and Dream 7B show moderate performance degradation on the Replace Index task as parallelism increases. However, Replace Random and Shuffle tasks, which have stronger token dependencies, exhibit significantly steeper accuracy drops with increased parallelism, with Shuffle being the most affected. See full paper for comprehensive 17-task analysis.

💡 Key Insight

Static unmasking methods (e.g., Top-K) show dramatic accuracy drops as parallelism increases, while adaptive methods (e.g., Threshold) achieve superior trade-offs. However, the oracle performance—using optimal per-sample thresholds—demonstrates that even the best current methods remain far from optimal, achieving both higher accuracy and greater speedup. This gap reveals substantial room for future research in adaptive unmasking strategies.

While most autoregressive LLMs are constrained to one-by-one decoding, diffusion LLMs (dLLMs) have attracted growing interest for their potential to dramatically accelerate inference through parallel decoding. Despite this promise, the conditional independence assumption in dLLMs causes parallel decoding to ignore token dependencies, inevitably degrading generation quality when these dependencies are strong. However, existing works largely overlook these inherent challenges, and evaluations on standard benchmarks (e.g., math and coding) are not sufficient to capture the quality degradation caused by parallel decoding. To address this gap, we first provide an information-theoretic analysis of parallel decoding. We then conduct case studies on analytically tractable synthetic list operations from both data distribution and decoding strategy perspectives, offering quantitative insights that highlight the fundamental limitations of parallel decoding. Building on these insights, we propose ParallelBench, the first benchmark specifically designed for dLLMs, featuring realistic tasks that are trivial for humans and autoregressive LLMs yet exceptionally challenging for dLLMs under parallel decoding. Using ParallelBench, we systematically analyze both dLLMs and autoregressive LLMs, revealing that: (i) dLLMs under parallel decoding can suffer dramatic quality degradation in real-world scenarios, and (ii) current parallel decoding strategies struggle to adapt their degree of parallelism based on task difficulty, thus failing to achieve meaningful speedup without compromising quality. Our findings underscore the pressing need for innovative decoding methods that can overcome the current speed-quality trade-off. We are releasing our benchmark to help accelerate the development of truly efficient dLLMs.
